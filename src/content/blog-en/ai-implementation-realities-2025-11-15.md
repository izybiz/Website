*This field report is grounded in operational work in West Africa. The 5 realities described — iteration speed, language barriers, team skills, infrastructure dependency — apply to any AI integration context under operational constraints.*

In November 2025, I decided to seriously integrate artificial intelligence into business operations in West Africa.

Not a POC, not an exploration. A real integration — across daily workflows: data analysis, content production, decision support on [interim management assignments](/management-de-transition), structuring client deliverables.

The result: in six weeks I learned more about the real state of AI in Africa than in two years of theoretical monitoring. And most of what I learned wasn't in any McKinsey article or IFC report.

Here are the five realities you'll encounter if you try the same thing.

---

## 1. The promise paradox: why AI looks accessible from the surface

The tools are there. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — accessible from any browser with an international payment card. The ambient narrative is optimistic: AI democratises access to knowledge, compensates for resource asymmetries, lets emerging economies "leapfrog" a development stage.

That's not wrong. But it's incomplete enough to be misleading.

Consumer tools mask a brutal structural reality: **Africa holds less than 1% of the global computing power dedicated to artificial intelligence**. That ratio doesn't just define a technological gap. It draws a sovereignty fracture — and a functional dependency that manifests very concretely the moment you move beyond surface-level productivity use cases.

---

## 2. The iteration wall: when delay becomes a structural impossibility

The first concrete friction I encountered wasn't the one I expected.

It wasn't a question of access or price. It was a question of experimentation speed.

Building a useful AI workflow — whether for analysing performance data or structuring a client diagnostic — requires testing, adjusting, retesting. It's a discipline of rapid iteration. In the UEMOA zone, unstable connections, latency from APIs hosted on North American or European servers, and the absence of local compute infrastructure create cumulative friction that makes this iteration exhausting.

There's a figure that struck me when I came across it: only 5% of African AI talent has sufficient access to computing power to work at a competitive pace. Where a tech team in Europe iterates on its models every 30 minutes, their counterpart in West Africa may wait six days to validate an equivalent change.

Six days versus 30 minutes. This isn't a setback. It's a structural impossibility of reaching real-time product-market fit.

For an [interim management assignment](/management-de-transition) where the first weeks are decisive, this friction matters. It forces you to design workflows differently — less real-time experimentation, more upfront architecture.

---

## 3. The models don't speak to your teams or your clients

The second friction is cultural and linguistic. It's more insidious than the first because it's invisible until it creates a real problem.

Large language models were trained predominantly on English data, with French as a secondary language in its standard form. Local variants — Ivorian French, Senegalese phrasing, communication codes specific to business environments in the sub-region — are almost entirely absent from training corpora.

The practical result: models produce outputs that sound right to a reader in Paris and strange to an interlocutor in Abidjan. For client deliverables or internal communications, this translates into systematic recontextualization work that the tool was supposed to eliminate.

At a deeper level: local languages are almost entirely absent. Africa is home to over 2,000 languages. Initiatives like Masakhane at the continental level, or the Awa project in Wolof in Senegal, are beginning to close this gap — but they haven't yet reached operational utility for a business. When your end client interacts in Dioula or Mooré, no mainstream model today helps you build an adapted product experience.

The scope of this linguistic gap — and its implications for [digital sovereignty in Africa](/en/blog/ai-africa-linguistic-gap) — is a structural issue, not a temporary bug.

---

## 4. The AI skills gap in African business teams

The third friction, I encountered not in the tools but in the teams expected to use them.

According to available data on the African labour market, **100% of organizations on the continent anticipate an AI skills shortage by 2025** — and 90% say these shortages are already causing delays in their innovation projects.

These figures match exactly what I observed.

The problem isn't enthusiasm. Teams want to use the tools. But between wanting to use ChatGPT to draft an email and being able to build an operational AI workflow that integrates into company processes, there's a skills gap that neither a two-day training nor a PowerPoint presentation will close.

This gap has a direct cost on [performance management assignments](/pilotage-performance): when AI is supposed to automate report production or anomaly detection in data, and the local team doesn't have the means to maintain those workflows independently, the value created evaporates the moment the assignment ends.

I drew one clear operational lesson from this: **any AI integration in an African context must include a non-negotiable skills transfer component.** The tool alone doesn't hold. The process alone doesn't hold. It's the combination of tool + process + team autonomy that holds.

---

## 5. Infrastructure: the invisible floor beneath any AI ambition

The fourth and fifth realities converge on the same floor: infrastructure.

AI consumes electricity and connectivity. That's not a metaphor. The most common inference models require stable connections and regular power supply to function reliably. Outside the capitals of the continent's most advanced countries, those conditions are not guaranteed.

Natalie Jabangwe of the Timbuktoo Foundation puts it directly: AI is inseparable from reliable electricity. That's not reductive. It's a technical prerequisite.

The emerging response — frugal AI, based on networks of small interconnected machines rather than massive GPUs — is interesting and promising. It represents a breakthrough innovation born of constraint. But it's not yet the standard infrastructure a company can rely on today for critical operations.

For [scale-up and industrialisation projects](/scale-up-industrialisation), this imposes a different design philosophy. AI workflows must be built to tolerate interruptions, operate in degraded mode, and never sit on the critical path of an operation that cannot stop.

## What works for AI integration in African business operations

| What you believe before | What you understand after |
|---|---|
| AI is accessible everywhere via the cloud | Local infrastructure conditions iteration speed |
| Tools work in all languages | Local variants and African languages are almost absent |
| Training teams in 2 days is enough | Skills transfer is a standalone mission lever |
| Models are culturally neutral | They reflect training data biases — mostly Western |
| AI reduces costs immediately | It creates value only if the workflow is maintained durably |

Africa is home to 18% of the world's population and controls less than 1% of the compute power dedicated to AI. That ratio won't change in 12 months. It defines the real conditions in which you'll be working.

What changes, however, is how you design the integration.

Rather than trying to replicate AI workflows designed for North American or European contexts, the approach that works is to start from real constraints — variable connectivity, limited skills, power dependency — and design solutions that are robust to those constraints from the start.

That's not a diminished ambition. It's a realistic one. And in a context where 90% of enterprise AI projects fail for lack of operational grounding, realism is the only competitive advantage that matters.
