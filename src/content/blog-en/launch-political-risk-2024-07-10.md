*This field report comes from a commercial launch in Senegal disrupted by the political crisis of February 2024. The lessons — local contact redundancy, calendar slack, connectivity as critical infrastructure — apply to any expansion project in a high country-risk market.*

In January 2024, we were in the final preparation stage to launch a commercial partnership in Senegal with a local operator, as part of the deployment of JIPS, the B2B platform I co-founded. Contract signed, operational processes aligned, launch date set for end of February. Political risk in West Africa was not in our plan's assumptions.

On February 3, 2024, Macky Sall appeared on national television.

In a matter of minutes, he announced the postponement of the presidential election scheduled for February 25 — a postponement the Constitutional Court would rule unconstitutional twelve days later. That evening, we didn't know that yet. What we knew was that our local partner had stopped answering our messages. And that images coming from Dakar showed crowds in the streets.

Our launch was compromised. Not because of a product problem. Not because of a flawed business model. Because of a political crisis that nobody had included in the launch plan's assumptions.

Here's what that experience taught me about country risk management on a digital project in West Africa.

---

## February 3: when political risk freezes your launch

To understand what happened, you need the context.

Macky Sall, in power since 2012, was reaching the end of his second constitutional term. The presidential election was set for February 25, 2024. Nineteen candidates were on the ballot, including Bassirou Diomaye Faye, secretary-general of PASTEF — the opposition movement of Ousmane Sonko, who had been imprisoned for nearly a year.

On the evening of February 3, Macky Sall announced the election postponement, citing a candidate eligibility dispute. The international reaction was immediate: the African Union, ECOWAS, and Western partners condemned it. The opposition called it a constitutional coup. Protests broke out in Dakar, Saint-Louis, and Ziguinchor.

For our local partner, priorities had shifted entirely. His family, his safety, his neighbourhood, the news cycling on the radio — none of it had any competition from a launch calendar.

This is not a criticism. It's the reality of what a historically unprecedented constitutional crisis means for a business owner whose network, family, and assets are all in the same place.

## Internet shutdowns: an invisible economic weapon

What political chronicles of this period rarely mention is the impact of connectivity restrictions on economic activity.

During the first weeks of February 2024, Senegalese authorities implemented targeted social media blocks and mobile internet slowdowns. TikTok, messaging platforms, and certain online services were disrupted. The organisation Netblocks documented several episodes of degraded Senegalese internet traffic between February 3 and 10.

For us, concretely: real-time coordination with our local partner became unpredictable. Collaboration tools worked intermittently. Document validations, meeting confirmations, decision loops — anything that required a stable connection — stretched out or fell into silence.

On a [digital platform launch project](/en/blog/jips-b2b-social-commerce-model-west-africa), this period coincides precisely with when execution is densest and communication with local teams most critical. Exactly when the communication infrastructure was becoming unreliable.

One data point to calibrate magnitude: according to available estimates for West Africa, a single day of internet disruption in an economy the size of Senegal represents between one and three million dollars in direct economic losses. For a small structure or a startup in launch phase, the cost in delayed decisions and operational friction is harder to quantify — but equally real.

## February 15: the decision that changed everything

Senegal's Constitutional Court issued a historic ruling on February 15, 2024.

It invalidated the postponement decided by Macky Sall and ordered the election to be held as soon as possible. To my knowledge, it was the first time an African high court had overturned a presidential decision of that magnitude in real time — and the president complied.

The election was set for March 24, 2024.

That ruling reopened a horizon. It didn't dissolve uncertainty — six weeks of a high-tension election campaign remained — but it gave a reference point. Our local partner started picking up the phone again. Discussions resumed, cautiously.

What I observed in the two weeks that followed: pure uncertainty paralyzed. The moment there was a date — even a loose one — economic actors started planning again. That reflex of conditional planning — *"if the election goes normally, then we resume in April"* — is what kept the project alive.

---

## March 14: the most improbable reversal

If the Constitutional Court decision was historic, what happened ten days before the election was genuinely unexpected.

Bassirou Diomaye Faye — the de facto opposition candidate, imprisoned since April 14, 2023, nearly a year — was released on March 14, 2024, under a general amnesty signed by Macky Sall. Ten days before polling day.

On March 24, 2024, he was elected President of the Republic of Senegal in the first round, with nearly 55% of the vote.

From prison to the Elysée in ten days. The kind of event that is a reminder that political scenarios in West Africa can take trajectories no conventional risk model anticipates.

For us, that election closed the uncertainty window. On April 2, 2024, Bassirou Diomaye Faye took office. A new administration, different priorities, a strong signal sent to foreign investors and partners about the country's institutional stability.

Our launch ultimately happened several weeks behind the original calendar.

## What this experience teaches about political risk in Africa

I don't draw from this episode the conclusion that you shouldn't launch in Senegal — or even that you shouldn't launch during an election period. Senegal remains one of the most institutionally stable environments in West Africa. The resilience its institutions demonstrated in February 2024 is proof of that.

What I take from it is more operational.

**Country risk — and political risk specifically — doesn't get managed through avoidance: it gets managed through project structure.** A launch plan with no calendar flexibility in an African election context is a fragile plan. Not because crises are inevitable, but because they're unpredictable in their timing and form.

**Dependence on a single local interlocutor is concentrated risk.** When our primary partner was absorbed by the crisis, we had no operational local relay. For the [interim management missions](/management-de-transition) I run on these markets, building a redundant local contact network is now a non-negotiable prerequisite.

**Connectivity is critical infrastructure, not a given.** Digital platforms that depend on mobile connectivity for their launch must incorporate degraded modes — partial offline functionality, async communication, simplified validation — into their technical architecture from the start.

**An unstable political environment is not a closed market.** Some of the strongest partnerships I've structured in West Africa were concluded during periods of tension — precisely because solid local actors are then seeking stable partnerships with reliable operators. Crises select for serious partners.

## What we'd have done differently

| Decision taken | What we'd do differently |
|---|---|
| Fixed launch date end of February | Flexible launch window, triggered conditionally on post-election stability |
| Coordination only through primary partner | Secondary local network identified upfront (lawyer, consultant, institutional contact) |
| Standard communication tools | Degraded communication protocol planned for connectivity gaps |
| Linear launch plan | Formal Plan A / Plan B with explicit trigger criteria |
| No country risk review in the dossier | Political review included in quarterly planning from the structuring phase |

[Scale-up on African markets](/scale-up-industrialisation) is a discipline of resilience as much as growth. The markets that offer the best opportunities are often those where institutions are still being built — and therefore those where unpredictable events are part of the normal operational landscape.

The question isn't to avoid those markets. It's to design projects robust enough to survive their turbulences.

This equation — structural resilience as much as performance — applies to any context where the environment changes faster than the plans: a market in deregulation, an industry in consolidation, a country in institutional transition. The political risk is never Africa specifically. It's structural unpredictability, wherever it manifests.
