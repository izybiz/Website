*This field report comes from finalizing an industrial partnership contract in West Africa. The contractual mechanisms described — pre-financing, personal guarantees, intuitu personae clauses — are applicable to any structured commercial partnership in an international context with high information asymmetry.*

I recently finalised a partnership agreement with a West African agri-food producer to structure the distribution of his production through a marketplace platform.

To structure it properly, I worked with a law firm specialising in Franco-African business law — one of the first decisions I recommend to anyone entering a significant industrial partnership on the continent. But through the exchanges with the lawyers and reading the final contract, I crystallised a conviction I had held for several years: **it isn't trust that secures an industrial partnership in Africa. It's structure.**

Here are the concrete mechanisms that make the difference — and why most poorly structured partnerships fail at the same point.

---

## Why industrial partnership structuring is decisive for a marketplace

When you operate a [marketplace in West Africa](/en/blog/jips-b2b-social-commerce-model-west-africa), the supplier relationship is not an ordinary commercial relationship.

The supplier isn't a service provider you can replace in 30 days. In agricultural commodities — rice, millet, cowpeas, palm oil — production cycles are long. Stocks are concentrated. Trust with local producers builds over years.

The result: the power dynamic is the reverse of what you find in European supply chains. The marketplace needs the producer as much as the other way around.

The partnership agreement must integrate this reality. The goal isn't to impose conditions. It's to create a structure of mutual interests where both parties have a rational reason to honour their commitments.

---

## Production pre-financing: aligning interests before the first kilogram

The first structural mechanism: campaign pre-financing.

The principle is simple. You pay a significant advance to the supplier before the production campaign, to allow them to acquire raw materials — paddy, agricultural inputs, milling capacity. In exchange, the entire production is sold through the platform.

Why this approach?

A mid-sized producer in the UEMOA zone (the West African economic union) typically has no access to bank credit at reasonable terms. Interest rates charged by local commercial banks often exceed 12 to 15%. As a result, producers sell their harvest at the lowest possible price, immediately after the harvest, because they cannot afford to store and wait.

By pre-financing the campaign at a rate below local banking alternatives, you create a double lever.

First, reciprocal economic dependency: the supplier has a rational interest in honouring the contract because the advance is tied to the production. Second, a progressive repayment mechanism — a fraction of monthly revenue goes toward amortising the advance — which aligns interests over the duration rather than creating a lump-sum repayment pressure.

This producer pre-financing structure is one of the most powerful models for structuring the platform/supplier relationship in Africa. Twiga Foods in Kenya has understood this for years with its smallholder farmers. It applies to any sector with long production cycles.

---

## Tiered commissions: how the contract shapes behaviour

The second mechanism: a tiered commission structure between retail and wholesale sales.

The retail channel generates more value for the platform and better margins for the supplier. The retail commission rate is therefore significantly higher than that applied to wholesale sales.

This rate differential creates a clear incentive: the supplier has an interest in directing sales toward the retail channel. Wholesale is reserved for clearing surpluses or maintaining the cash flow needed for production — and is decided by mutual agreement.

This is a critical point: **channel exclusivity isn't a constraint imposed on the supplier — it's mutual protection.** The supplier doesn't scatter his stock across multiple channels with inconsistent pricing. The platform controls pricing policy and guarantees retail competitiveness.

In a sector as price-sensitive as consumer food, this is a structural necessity.

---

## Operational guarantees: don't leave timelines to goodwill

The third pillar: operational commitments contractualised with penalties.

A solid distribution contract specifies:
- The order delivery timeframe for in-stock items after placement on the platform
- The warehouse replenishment timeframe in case of stockout
- Collection time windows
- The penalty applicable in case of delay, calculated on the pre-tax order value

These service levels are not theoretical. In a consumer e-commerce context, the tolerance of the urban African consumer for delivery delays is low. An unmanaged delay translates directly into cancellations, refunds, and platform reputation damage.

What I've learned about industrial partnerships in Africa — and what many platforms discover at their cost — is that an un-contractualised SLA doesn't exist. Goodwill lasts for the first three months. After that, daily pressure, transport uncertainties, and production capacity issues take over.

Contractualising timelines with real penalties is the only way to build lasting logistics discipline.

## The personal guarantee: a critical mechanism in the African context

The fourth mechanism is the one I consider most underestimated by foreign operators entering West Africa: **the personal guarantee of the principal.**

The structural risk with a recently incorporated company under local law is that its capital and assets may be insufficient to cover counterparty risk. In the event of default on the advance repayment, a recovery action against the company itself can take years and result in partial recovery.

A personal guarantee from the principal shareholder radically changes the equation. The guarantor pledges their personal income and assets, waives the benefit of excussion (the right to require that the company's assets be exhausted first), and agrees to pay immediately in case of default — without the platform needing to pursue the company first.

This mechanism is close to the Anglo-Saxon personal guarantee, rarely used in Franco-African commercial partnerships even though it is completely standard in equivalent transactions in Kenya, Nigeria, or South Africa.

If you are structuring a partnership with a significant financial contribution to a thinly capitalised company in the UEMOA or CEMAC zone, the personal guarantee of the director or principal shareholder is non-negotiable. Not as a signal of distrust — as a condition for a credible commitment.

## The intuitu personae clause: protecting the partnership from ownership changes

The fifth mechanism: the *intuitu personae* clause.

It stipulates that the contract was entered into in consideration of the personality of the directors, the capital structure, and the guarantor. Consequently:

- Any assignment, transfer, or transmission requires the prior written consent of the other party
- If the guarantor or a key director leaves the structure, the platform may terminate the contract without indemnity

This clause protects against a classic scenario in a growth context: the supplier is taken over, sold, or transferred to a third party. The conditions negotiated with the original management don't automatically transfer to the new owner. Without an intuitu personae clause, you can find yourself bound long-term to an unknown partner with divergent interests.

This risk is particularly relevant in African agri-food sectors, where consolidation among players is rapid and often opaque to outside partners.

---

## The ambassador model: beyond the buyer-supplier relationship

One final mechanism rarely discussed in conventional distribution models: the contractualised ambassador role.

The supplier commits to actively promoting the platform among their regular clients and among other producers in the region. They also contribute to the joint promotional campaign budget.

This mechanism transforms the relationship. The supplier is no longer a simple stock provider. They become an ecosystem actor with a financial and commercial interest in the platform's growth.

For a retail marketplace in Africa, supplier acquisition is as critical a challenge as customer acquisition. Each producer enrolled in the ambassador model becomes an acquisition channel at near-zero marginal cost. This is a [growth structuring lever](/scale-up-industrialisation) that successful African platforms integrate systematically.

## The clauses not to overlook in negotiation

Before reaching the signing table, here are the nine points I systematically verify in any industrial partnership contract in West Africa:

**On financial commitments**
- Pre-financing mechanism with rate, repayment schedule, and local tax exemption conditions
- Identification of a solvent personal guarantor on the supplier side, with waiver of the benefit of excussion
- Tiered commissions by sales channel to align incentives

**On operational commitments**
- Delivery SLA with precise timelines and penalties calculated on pre-tax order value
- Product traceability obligation: barcodes, regulatory compliance, guaranteed minimum shelf life
- Product listing procedure on the platform before any commercial transaction

**On partnership structure**
- Intuitu personae clause with termination conditions if management or capital changes
- Ban on assignment or transfer without prior written consent
- Mandatory professional liability and product insurance, with certificate submitted within an agreed deadline
- Use of a law firm specialised in Franco-African business law for drafting

These aren't legal details. They are the mechanisms that will — or will not — allow you to manage the inevitable frictions during execution.

---

## What I take away for building durable industrial partnerships in Africa

| What doesn't work | What actually secures the deal |
|---|---|
| Verbal agreement or MOU without precise obligations | Contract with SLA, penalties, and exit mechanisms |
| Dependence on the founder's goodwill | Personal guarantee + intuitu personae clause |
| Flat commission regardless of channel | Tiered commissions to orient behaviour |
| Advance without progressive repayment mechanism | Monthly amortisation on revenue + contractual interest |
| Passive supplier | Ambassador supplier with budget contribution |
| Contract drafted in-house without local expertise | Specialist firm in Franco-African business law |

In 30 years, I have seen dozens of strategic partnerships derail. In the vast majority of cases, the problem wasn't the parties' willingness at the outset. It was the absence of a contractual structure capable of holding when things get difficult — delayed campaigns, stockouts, management changes, margin pressure.

A well-structured contract doesn't replace the relationship. It protects it.
